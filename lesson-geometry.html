📐 Angle Relationships

Angles are formed when two rays share a common endpoint (called the vertex). Understanding how angles relate to each other is the foundation of geometry.

Key Concept

A straight line = 180°. A full rotation = 360°. These two facts power almost every angle problem.

Types of Angles

  • Acute: less than 90°
  • Right: exactly 90° (marked with a square symbol)
  • Obtuse: between 90° and 180°
  • Straight: exactly 180°
  • Reflex: between 180° and 360°

Angle Pair Relationships

  • Complementary: two angles that add to 90°
  • Supplementary: two angles that add to 180°
  • Vertical angles: opposite angles formed by two intersecting lines — always equal
  • Linear pair: adjacent angles on a straight line — always supplementary
✏️ Worked Example — Supplementary Angles
Two angles are supplementary. One angle measures 65°. Find the other.
Supplementary angles add up to 180°. Subtract the known angle from 180°.
  1. Supplementary means they sum to 180°
  2. Set up the equation:   65° + x = 180°
  3. Subtract 65° from both sides:   x = 180° − 65°
  4. x = 115°
65° + x = 180° x = 180° − 65° x = 115°
✏️ Worked Example — Vertical Angles
Two lines intersect. One angle is (3x + 10)°. Its vertical angle is (5x − 20)°. Find x.
Vertical angles are equal. Set the two expressions equal to each other and solve.
  1. Vertical angles are equal:   3x + 10 = 5x − 20
  2. Subtract 3x from both sides:   10 = 2x − 20
  3. Add 20 to both sides:   30 = 2x
  4. Divide by 2:   x = 15
  5. Check: 3(15)+10 = 55°, 5(15)−20 = 55° ✅
3x + 10 = 5x − 20 30 = 2x x = 15

🔺 Triangles

Triangles are the most fundamental polygon. Their properties appear in architecture, art, engineering, and of course — math exams.

Key Concept

The three interior angles of any triangle always add up to exactly 180°. This is called the Triangle Angle Sum Theorem.

Types of Triangles

By sides:

  • Equilateral: all 3 sides equal, all angles = 60°
  • Isosceles: 2 sides equal, base angles are equal
  • Scalene: all sides different, all angles different

By angles:

  • Acute: all angles less than 90°
  • Right: one angle exactly 90° (use Pythagorean theorem!)
  • Obtuse: one angle greater than 90°
✏️ Worked Example 1 — Missing Angle
A triangle has angles of 47° and 83°. Find the third angle.
All three angles must add up to 180°. Add the two known angles, then subtract from 180°.
  1. Angles sum to 180°:   47° + 83° + x = 180°
  2. Add known angles:   130° + x = 180°
  3. Subtract:   x = 50°
47° + 83° + x = 180° 130° + x = 180° x = 50°
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Pythagorean Theorem
A right triangle has legs of 6 and 8. Find the hypotenuse.
Use a² + b² = c² where c is the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle).
  1. Write the formula:   a² + b² = c²
  2. Substitute:   6² + 8² = c²
  3. Calculate:   36 + 64 = c²
  4. 100 = c²  →  c = √100 = 10
a² + b² = c² 6² + 8² = c² 36 + 64 = c² c² = 100 c = 10

📏 Area & Perimeter

Perimeter is the distance around the outside of a shape. Area is the amount of space inside it. Always check your units — perimeter uses units (cm), area uses square units (cm²).

Key Formulas

Rectangle: P = 2(l + w), A = l × w  |  Triangle: A = ½ × b × h  |  Circle: C = 2πr, A = πr²

Common Formulas

Rectangle Perimeter = 2(length + width) Area = length × width Triangle Area = ½ × base × height Circle Circumference = 2 × π × radius Area = π × radius²
✏️ Worked Example 1 — Rectangle
A rectangle is 12 cm long and 5 cm wide. Find its perimeter and area.
Use P = 2(l + w) for perimeter and A = l × w for area.
  1. Perimeter:   P = 2(12 + 5) = 2(17) = 34 cm
  2. Area:   A = 12 × 5 = 60 cm²
P = 2(12 + 5) = 2(17) = 34 cm A = 12 × 5 = 60 cm²
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Circle
A circle has a radius of 7 cm. Find its circumference and area. (Use π ≈ 3.14)
C = 2πr and A = πr². Substitute r = 7 and π = 3.14.
  1. Circumference:   C = 2 × 3.14 × 7 = 43.96 cm
  2. Area:   A = 3.14 × 7² = 3.14 × 49 = 153.86 cm²
C = 2 × 3.14 × 7 = 43.96 cm A = 3.14 × 49 = 153.86 cm²
