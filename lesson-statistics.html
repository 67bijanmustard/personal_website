📊 Mean & Median

When working with data, we often want one number that represents the "center" of the data. The two most common measures are the mean and the median.

Key Concept

Mean = average (sum ÷ count). Median = middle value when data is sorted. Use median when there are extreme outliers that would skew the mean.

Calculating the Mean

Add all the values together, then divide by how many values there are.

Mean = (sum of all values) ÷ (number of values)

Finding the Median

  • Sort the data from smallest to largest
  • If there's an odd number of values, the median is the middle one
  • If there's an even number of values, average the two middle numbers
✏️ Worked Example — Mean
Find the mean:   4, 8, 6, 10, 7, 3, 6
Add all seven numbers together, then divide by 7.
  1. Count the values: there are 7 numbers
  2. Add them: 4 + 8 + 6 + 10 + 7 + 3 + 6 = 44
  3. Divide by count: 44 ÷ 7 ≈ 6.29
Sum = 4+8+6+10+7+3+6 = 44 Mean = 44 ÷ 7 = 6.29
✏️ Worked Example — Median (Even Count)
Find the median:   12, 5, 9, 3, 7, 14
Sort first! Then with 6 values (even), average the 3rd and 4th values.
  1. Sort ascending: 3, 5, 7, 9, 12, 14
  2. 6 values (even) → average the 3rd and 4th: 7 and 9
  3. Median = (7 + 9) ÷ 2 = 16 ÷ 2 = 8
Sorted: 3, 5, 7, 9, 12, 14 Median = (7 + 9) ÷ 2 = 8

📈 Mode & Range

Two more tools for describing data: the mode tells you what value appears most often, and the range tells you how spread out the data is.

Key Concept

Mode = most frequent value (a data set can have no mode, one mode, or multiple modes). Range = largest value − smallest value.

Quick Tips

  • A data set is unimodal if it has one mode
  • A data set is bimodal if it has two modes
  • If no value repeats, there is no mode
  • Range measures spread, not center — a large range means data is widely scattered
✏️ Worked Example — Mode & Range
Find the mode and range:   5, 3, 8, 3, 9, 5, 3, 7
Tally how many times each number appears. For range, subtract the smallest from the largest.
  1. Tally: 3 appears 3 times, 5 appears 2 times, others once
  2. Mode = 3 (appears most — 3 times)
  3. Range = largest − smallest = 9 − 3 = 6
Tally: 3→3×, 5→2×, 7→1×, 8→1×, 9→1× Mode = 3 Range = 9 − 3 = 6
✏️ Worked Example — All Four Measures
Data set: 2, 6, 4, 6, 8, 4, 6, 10. Find mean, median, mode, and range.
Sort first: 2, 4, 4, 6, 6, 6, 8, 10. Then apply each formula.
  1. Sorted: 2, 4, 4, 6, 6, 6, 8, 10
  2. Mean: (2+4+4+6+6+6+8+10) ÷ 8 = 46 ÷ 8 = 5.75
  3. Median: (6+6) ÷ 2 = 6 (middle two values)
  4. Mode: 6 (appears 3 times)
  5. Range: 10 − 2 = 8
Mean = 46 ÷ 8 = 5.75 Median = (6+6) ÷ 2 = 6 Mode = 6 Range = 10 − 2 = 8

🎲 Probability

Probability measures how likely an event is to occur. It is always a number between 0 (impossible) and 1 (certain), or expressed as a percentage between 0% and 100%.

Key Formula

P(event) = Number of favorable outcomes ÷ Total number of possible outcomes

Types of Events

  • Simple event: one outcome (e.g. rolling a 4)
  • Compound event: two or more outcomes (e.g. rolling a 4 OR a 5)
  • Complementary event: P(not A) = 1 − P(A)

Probability Scale

0 ──────────── 0.5 ──────────── 1 Impossible 50/50 Certain
✏️ Worked Example 1 — Simple Probability
A bag has 3 red, 4 blue, and 5 green marbles. What is the probability of picking a blue marble?
Total outcomes = 3+4+5 = 12. Favorable outcomes = 4 (blue marbles).
  1. Total marbles: 3 + 4 + 5 = 12
  2. Favorable outcomes (blue): 4
  3. P(blue) = 4/12 = 1/3 ≈ 0.333 = 33.3%
P(blue) = 4 ÷ 12 = 1/3 ≈ 33.3%
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Complementary Events
The probability of rain tomorrow is 0.35. What is the probability it will NOT rain?
P(not A) = 1 − P(A). The two complementary events must add to 1 (100%).
  1. P(rain) = 0.35
  2. P(not rain) = 1 − 0.35 = 0.65
  3. There is a 65% chance it will not rain.
P(not rain) = 1 − 0.35 = 0.65 (65%)
